Our end goal is transparency: communities want to be involved and learn about carbon credit funds and decision-making, but current methods are absent.

Bidirectional, bottom-up communication is the foundational solution that enables future social objectives.

Bidirectional communication channels

Community members should have inputs upstream towards overseeing governance structures, voicing their perspectives and pressing needs.

Inclusion of marginalized communities, vulnerable populations and women in decision-making

Adopting a contestable, verifiable, easily understood version of existing Social CoMMs surveys to ensure inclusive representation of groups with low current participation levels.

Comprehensive transparency in the carbon credit creation process

Carbon credit proceeds and market transactions are understood at all levels, especially community members who are informed and empowered to leverage climate finance.

How it works

Digital tools let community members give faster, direct feedback to partners like AIID Africa and Wowzi (surveys, updates). Implementing agencies manage data for integrity and action. Educational materials on carbon processes are then refined and shared back through the same streams.

  1. SMS / IVR — Community reports confusion about carbon funds.
  2. SCOUT — Flags low carbon credit knowledge, income control; identifies gender gap.
  3. Implementing agency — Responds (logged).
  4. Education — Module via SMS/IVR; summarized on dashboard.
  5. Dashboard — Shows issue, response time, education delivered, follow-up.

Empowerment dimensions

SCOUT maps community inputs to these dimensions. ILRI – Women’s Empowerment in Livestock Index.

  1. Decisions about agricultural production
  2. Decisions related to nutrition
  3. Access to and control over resources
  4. Control and use of income
  5. Access to and control of opportunities
  6. Workload and control over own time
